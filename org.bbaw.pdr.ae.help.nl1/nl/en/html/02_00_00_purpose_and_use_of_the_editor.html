Purpose and use of the Editor

Exactly how biographical information is documented differs from project to project. Generally, biographical information is classified through a standard system: “name”, “birth”, “death”, or “profession” are examples of the most common kinds of data recorded about persons. “place of study”, “spouse” or “confession”, are less common, and “reason for exile”, “musical education”, or “political affiliation” are seldom used. In order to retain this plurality, a data model has been developed that assimilates multiple kinds of data while still allowing comparisons between them. Similarly, the challenge of variant names and dates has also been considered in the development of the data model.The current data model comprehends a person, in their biographical sense, as a summation of descriptions. One short description can already constitute a person; a person who is not described, however, does not yet exist. If multiple descriptions exist, each one contains a certain innate value, even when they contradict one another.Thus the smallest unit of information which could be found in the data model would be a single expression about a person. This kind of single expression will be referred to as an “aspect”. The core of an aspect is a short descriptive text, such as a sentence, phrase, or a single word. This descriptive text, also referred to in the data model as a “notification”, is additionally marked up with semantic tags. In the Archiv-Editor this markup is displayed with corresponding colors.

For example:

The composerGeorg Friedrich Händelwas born in Hallein 1685 .

It is possible to compare aspects due to their internal structure.

The composerJohann Sebastian Bachwas born in Eisenachin 1685 .

One could then, for example, search for all composers who were born within the region Sachsen between 1670 and 1680.
